# Sutherland — Psycho-Logic Reference for Comms Strategists

*Source: **Alchemy: The Dark Art and Curious Science of Creating Magic in Brands, Business and Life**, Rory Sutherland (2019)*

---

## The Core Argument

Logic optimises for what can be measured. Psycho-logic optimises for what people actually feel. Most corporate strategy — including most communications strategy — is written by people trained to think logically, for audiences who think psycho-logically.

> *"The opposite of a good idea can be a good idea."*

The implication: counterintuitive solutions are often the most effective ones, because the logical solution has already been tried by everyone else. The creative opportunity in strategy is precisely where logic runs out.

---

## The GPS Parable

A GPS knows everything about roads and nothing about the driver. It will confidently recommend the *technically fastest* route — which is not the same as the *best* route for a person with a flight to catch, a preference for scenic drives, or an aversion to motorways.

This is the central metaphor for rational strategy applied to irrational humans: technically correct, psychologically blind.

**The strategic implication:** Before asking "what is the optimal solution?", ask "what does the optimal solution *feel like* to the person receiving it?" These are different questions with different answers.

## The Four S's — Framework Reference

### 1. Signalling

Signals communicate information *indirectly* through observable behaviour or attributes that are costly to fake. Trust, quality, and commitment are communicated not through claims, but through *costly signals*.

**Key principle — Costly Signalling:**
A signal is credible only if it is *expensive to produce*. Cheap signals (verbal claims, unverifiable certifications) are discounted. Expensive signals (premium design, physical stores, long-term presence) are believed because they require a real investment that a bad actor couldn't sustain.

| Costly signal | What it communicates |
|--------------|---------------------|
| High production values | Investment and commitment |
| Physical presence (store, office) | Permanence and accountability |
| Unconditional guarantees | Confidence in the product |
| Slow process (waiting list) | Desirability and selectivity |
| Premium price | Quality and exclusivity |
| Age / heritage | Survival and proven value |

> *"Advertising does not always look like advertising. A brand that builds an expensive flagship store, sponsors a major sporting event, or plants an orchard in a public square is signalling commitment through non-verbal means that no press release can replicate."*

**Strategic implication:** What costly signals is your brand producing? If all your signals are verbal (we claim to be good, we say we care), they are discountable. Design for costly signals.

### 2. Subconscious Hacking

Much of what influences a decision happens below the level of conscious awareness. Packaging weight, ambient sound, colour temperature, spatial design, typography weight — all create impressions that precede and often override the message.

> *"The conscious mind thinks it's the Oval Office, when in reality it's the Press Office."*  
> The conscious mind rationalises decisions that have already been made subconsciously.

**The placebo principle in marketing:**
The *expectation* of an experience shapes the *experience*. A wine presented as expensive tastes better — not just seems better, but *neurologically* produces more pleasure. The expectation creates the reality.

Implications for comms:
- Price communicates quality before the product is experienced
- Brand reputation creates the expectation that shapes satisfaction
- The channel you choose signals what kind of communication this is — long before the message is read

**What subconscious hacking looks and feels like:**
- The sound of a luxury car door closing (engineered to feel substantial)
- The weight of a premium credit card (titanium vs plastic)
- The smell of a high-end retailer (scent marketing)
- The difficulty of unwrapping an Apple product (the experience signals care)
- The font weight on a pharmaceutical label (lighter = more trustworthy; heavier = more powerful)

**Strategic question for any brief:** What are we asking the audience to *feel* before they receive any message? Is the context, format, and sensory environment aligned with the claimed positioning?

### 3. Satisficing

People don't optimise. They *satisfice* — they search for a solution that is *good enough* given their constraints (time, information, energy), and stop when they find one.

> *"He's not stupid, he's satisficing."*

The brand is a satisficing device. It removes the cognitive load of re-evaluating every purchase. The buyer of a familiar brand is not lazy — they are *efficient*. They have outsourced the evaluation process to their past experience.

**Satisficing implications for strategy:**

| Insight | Application |
|---------|-------------|
| Reduce decision complexity | Fewer options, clearer recommendation, simpler pricing |
| Remove perceived risk | Guarantee, trial, money-back, social proof |
| Make the default the right choice | Subscription, auto-renew, pre-ticked boxes |
| Compete with "good enough", not "best" | Being familiar and available beats being superior but unknown |

**Warning for challenger brands:** It is not enough to be better. You must also be *easier to choose*. The incumbent brand benefits from satisficing — it requires no effort to re-choose. The challenger must make *switching* feel like satisficing, not optimising.

> *"JFK vs EWR: the best airport is not always the least worst option."*  
> What people actually want is not necessarily the objectively superior option — it's the one that minimises their worst-case scenario (variance), not the one that maximises their average outcome.

### 4. Psychophysics — The Weber-Fechner Law

Human perception is *logarithmic*, not linear. We respond to proportional changes, not absolute changes. A 10-gram weight difference is noticeable on a 20-gram object but imperceptible on a 5-kilogram object.

**Implications for pricing and value communication:**

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| "We reduced the price by €5" | "We reduced the price by 25%" (if the product costs €20) |
| Presenting a small absolute gain | Present as a large relative gain |
| Adding small features to an expensive product | The customer won't notice — the proportional addition is too small |

**The map is not the territory — but the map shapes experience:**
> *"The packaging is the product."*

People experience products through the lens of what they expect. A beautiful package, a well-designed website, an elegant store environment doesn't merely influence *preference* — it changes the *experience* of the product itself.

## Sutherland's Diagnostic Questions

Ask these questions at any point in strategy development:

**1. What is the psycho-logical solution?**
If everyone is applying the same logical solution, the creative white space is in the psycho-logical solution. What would be the opposite of the obvious answer — and could it work?

**2. What is the real why?**
Surface motivations are rarely the operative ones. What would the audience be embarrassed to admit actually drives them? What fear is the purchase designed to manage?

**3. What costly signal are we producing?**
What does the brand *do* (not say) that proves its claims? What behaviours demonstrate commitment in a way that words cannot?

**4. Are we making it easier to choose us?**
Not better — *easier*. What friction in the decision or purchase process are we removing? What cognitive load are we taking away?

**5. What is the context before the message?**
What does the audience feel *before* they engage with the message? What primes their reception? What does the channel itself signal?

**6. What would happen if we did the opposite?**
In domains where everyone is doing the same thing, the anomalous choice is often the differentiating one. What is the counterintuitive version of this strategy?

## Rory's Rules of Alchemy (Condensed)

1. **Don't design for an average.** Averages mask the motivations of everyone.
2. **Be careful with maths.** The need to look rational can make you act dumb.
3. **A change in perspective is worth 80 IQ points.** Reframing changes the value of what already exists.
4. **The right answer for the wrong reason is still the right answer.** Don't dismiss a solution because you can't fully explain it.
5. **Context is everything.** Same thing, different context = different value, different meaning.
6. **Efficiency and meaning are often opposed.** What can be optimised away often contains what people value most.
7. **Find different expressions for the same thing.** "Economy class" sounds worse than "coach." Semantics create value.
8. **Create gratuitous choices.** Choice creates ownership. Agency creates satisfaction.
9. **Always remember to scent the soap.** The detail that doesn't serve a logical purpose often serves the most important psychological purpose.

## Application: The Psycho-Logic Brief

When a brief is too rational, add a psycho-logic layer:

```
PSYCHO-LOGIC BRIEF ADDENDUM

Real Why:
[What the audience is actually managing / avoiding / seeking, beneath the stated motivation]

Costly Signal Required:
[What behaviour or expression (not claim) will make the brand credible]

Satisficing Play:
[How we make choosing us feel like zero effort]

Subconscious Layer:
[What we want the audience to feel before they think — and what environment/format creates that feeling]

Counterintuitive Option:
[The solution that logic would dismiss but psycho-logic suggests worth testing]
```
