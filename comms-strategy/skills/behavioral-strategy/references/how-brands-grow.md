# Byron Sharp — How Brands Grow: Reference for Comms Strategists

*Source: **How Brands Grow: What Marketers Don't Know**, Byron Sharp (2010)*  
*Research base: Ehrenberg-Bass Institute for Marketing Science*

> *Note: This reference draws on Sharp's published research and findings. The book itself is not among the uploaded documents but its framework is foundational to modern evidence-based brand strategy.*

---

## The Core Thesis

Most marketing strategy is built on assumptions that empirical research consistently contradicts. How Brands Grow presents the counter-evidence — and a model for growth that follows the actual patterns in purchase data rather than intuitive but incorrect beliefs.

> *"Brand loyalty is a symptom of market share, not its cause."*

---

## The Fundamental Laws of Brand Growth

### Law 1: Double Jeopardy

Small brands suffer on two dimensions simultaneously:
- Fewer buyers
- Lower loyalty among those buyers

This is not a fixable problem through better CRM. It is a structural consequence of market size. The only way to escape double jeopardy is to grow penetration — acquire more buyers.

**Implication:** Strategies that focus on deepening loyalty without growing penetration are not neutral — they are slow decline. Every brand needs a constant flow of new buyers just to maintain its base (natural churn).

---

### Law 2: Growth Comes from Penetration, Not Loyalty

The primary driver of brand growth is increasing the number of people who buy the brand — including infrequent and first-time buyers.

| Assumption | Evidence |
|-----------|---------|
| Heavy users (superfans) drive growth | Heavy users are a small, naturally limited segment |
| Loyalty programmes grow brands | They retain existing buyers; they rarely attract new ones |
| Focus on core audience to maximise ROI | Core audience is already buying — the growth pool is everyone else |

**Light buyers are the growth pool:**
In virtually every category studied, the majority of a brand's sales come from light buyers — people who buy infrequently and also buy competitors. They are harder to reach, easier to lose, and responsible for most of the variance in sales.

**Strategic implication:** Comms that only "speaks to the brand's fans" is systematically under-serving the buyers who represent most of the growth opportunity.

---

### Law 3: Mental Availability

Mental availability is the probability that a brand will come to mind in a buying situation. It is distinct from awareness (knowing the brand exists) and distinct from preference (wanting the brand above others).

Mental availability is built through:
1. **Reach** — being seen by a large proportion of category buyers
2. **Freshness** — being seen recently enough to be salient
3. **Distinctive assets** — being recognisable through consistent brand codes
4. **Category Entry Points** — being linked to the situations in which people buy

> *"The goal of advertising is not persuasion — it is to maintain and refresh memory structures that make a brand come to mind at the moment of purchase."*

---

### Law 4: Physical Availability

The brand that is easier to find and buy grows more than the brand that is preferred but harder to access. Physical availability includes:
- Distribution (number of outlets or digital touchpoints)
- Visibility within those outlets (shelf positioning, digital prominence)
- Ease of purchase (fewer clicks, faster checkout, simpler subscription)

**The comms implication:** Physical availability amplifies mental availability. A strong brand with weak distribution loses. Never plan a comms campaign without checking the physical availability context.

---

### Law 5: Distinctive Assets vs Differentiation

Sharp distinguishes two commonly confused concepts:

| Distinctive Assets | Differentiation |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Brand codes that make the brand recognisable | Unique features that make the brand superior |
| Owned by memory and association | Owned by product/service reality |
| Built through consistent repetition | Built through R&D and positioning claims |
| Purpose: *recognition* | Purpose: *preference* |
| Sharp: **highly important** | Sharp: **often overstated** |

Most brands over-invest in differentiation (proving they are different or better) and under-invest in distinctiveness (ensuring they are *recognised*).

**Distinctive assets include:**
- Colour palette (Tiffany blue, Hermès orange, Barclays blue)
- Brand characters (the Michelin Man, Tony the Tiger)
- Sonic identity (Intel inside jingle, McDonald's "da da da da daa")
- Visual style (Penguin book spines, Apple minimalism)
- Taglines (used consistently over decades, not changed for every campaign)

**The asset destruction problem:**
Campaigns that change the visual identity, retire brand characters, or "refresh" logos in the name of modernity systematically destroy accumulated distinctive assets. The new assets have zero memory value on day one.

---

### Category Entry Points (CEPs) — Practical Framework

CEPs are the mental hooks — situations, needs, and triggers — through which category buyers enter the purchase process. A brand must be linked to CEPs to be retrieved.

**How to map CEPs:**

| Dimension | Questions |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Occasion** | When do people buy? (morning, evening, weekend, at work) |
| **Need state** | What problem are they solving? (hungry, bored, stressed, celebrating) |
| **Social context** | Alone or with others? Who else is involved in the decision? |
| **Environment** | Where are they? (commuting, at home, in a meeting, shopping) |
| **Channel** | How are they buying? (impulse in-store, planned online, subscription) |

**CEP linking strategy:**
For each priority CEP, ask:
1. Is our brand currently linked to this CEP in buyers' memories?
2. How could this campaign strengthen that link?
3. Are we showing the category occasion/need state as well as the brand?

### Reach vs Targeting: The Evidence

Sharp's research challenges the conventional case for precision targeting:

| Targeting assumption | Evidence-based challenge |
|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Target only high-propensity buyers | High-propensity buyers are already buying — they don't need persuasion |
| Avoid current users (already converted) | Current users need re-activation and salience maintenance |
| Deep targeting is more efficient | Narrow targeting under-reaches light buyers — the growth pool |
| Personalisation drives loyalty | Loyalty varies little regardless of personalisation investment |

**Sharp's prescription:** Maximise reach across all category buyers. A campaign seen by 10 million people once is often more valuable than a campaign seen by 100,000 people 100 times.

**Note:** This doesn't mean targeting is worthless — it means the *objective* of targeting should be *reach across the category* rather than *depth with a narrow segment*.

## Application: The Brand Growth Audit

Use to pressure-test any campaign brief against Sharp's framework:

```
BRAND GROWTH AUDIT

Mental Availability
- Which CEPs does this campaign link the brand to? List 3 max.
- Are we using Distinctive Assets consistently? [Yes / Partially / No]
- What % of category buyers will this campaign reach?
- Will it reach light buyers and non-buyers? [Yes / No / Unknown]

Physical Availability
- Is distribution adequate to support this campaign?
- Is the brand visible enough at point of purchase / digital shelf?

Penetration Goal
- Is this campaign designed to attract new buyers or deepen loyalty with existing ones?
- [If loyalty-focused]: Is this explicitly a retention play — and does the business case support it?

Distinctive Asset Health
- List the brand's top 3 distinctive assets: [Asset 1], [Asset 2], [Asset 3]
- Are all three present and consistent in this campaign?
- Is this campaign adding to or eroding these assets?

Red Flags
- Is the campaign only speaking to the brand's most loyal users?
- Is it changing visual assets that have been built over time?
- Is it trying to be "different" in ways that reduce recognisability?
```

## The Ehrenberg-Bass Principles in One Page

| Principle | Practical rule |
|-----------|---------------|
| Growth = Penetration | Always be recruiting new buyers, even in a "loyalty" campaign |
| Light buyers > heavy users | Design comms for the occasional buyer, not the superfan |
| Mental availability > differentiation | Be remembered first; be preferred second |
| Distinctive assets > refreshed assets | Never retire what has built recognition |
| Reach > depth | Broad reach across the category beats deep engagement with a narrow segment |
| CEPs anchor memory | Always show when/where/why people use the product — not just what it is |
| Consistent > consistent campaigns | The brand itself must be consistent; individual campaigns can vary |
