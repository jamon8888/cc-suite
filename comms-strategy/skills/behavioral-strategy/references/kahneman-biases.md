# Kahneman — Cognitive Bias Reference for Comms Strategists

*Source: **Thinking Fast and Slow**, Daniel Kahneman (2011)*

This reference file documents the cognitive biases from Kahneman's work most relevant to communications strategy. Each entry includes the mechanism, the strategic implication, and a worked example for comms.

---

## The Architecture: System 1 and System 2

Kahneman's central model distinguishes two cognitive systems that operate in parallel:

**System 1** — Fast, automatic, effortless. Generates impressions, intuitions, feelings. Cannot be turned off. Operates by association, pattern recognition, and heuristics. Responsible for the vast majority of judgments and decisions.

**System 2** — Slow, deliberate, effortful. Handles analysis, reasoning, and complex calculations. Rarely engaged. Tends to accept System 1's impressions as true unless there's a strong reason to override them.

> *"System 2 is not an independent judge — it is mostly a confirmer of what System 1 has already concluded."*

**The comms implication:** A message that requires System 2 to engage is a message that most people will not process. Design for System 1 first.

## Bias Library

### 1. Cognitive Ease

**Mechanism:** Things that are easy to process — familiar, clearly written, legible, regularly encountered — are judged as *true*, *good*, and *safe*. Things that are hard to process feel *suspicious*, *risky*, or *unpleasant* — regardless of their actual content.

**In comms:** Simple language is not dumbing down — it is a trust signal. A complex sentence structure makes a claim *feel less true*, even if it is more accurate. Familiarity breeds comfort — which is why consistent brand assets (colours, fonts, tone) accumulate value over time.

**Test question:** Does this feel fluent? Would someone reading it feel slightly relieved, or slightly tense?

---

### 2. Anchoring

**Mechanism:** The first piece of numerical information encountered becomes the *reference point* against which all subsequent information is evaluated — even if the first number is completely arbitrary.

**In comms:**
- Lead with the original price before the sale price
- Frame a premium price next to a comparable luxury item, not a cheaper alternative
- Use large achievement numbers early ("trusted by 2 million professionals") before introducing costs

**Warning:** Anchoring works even when people *know* the anchor is arbitrary. Knowing the trick doesn't neutralise it.

---

### 3. Framing Effects

**Mechanism:** Logically equivalent statements, when framed differently, produce different decisions. People respond to *gains* (approach motivation) and *losses* (avoidance motivation) through different psychological mechanisms.

| Frame | When to use |
|-------|-------------|
| **Gain frame** ("You'll save €200") | Aspirational contexts, luxury, new behaviour adoption |
| **Loss frame** ("You'll lose €200 if you don't act") | High-stakes decisions, health, security, risk management |
| **Relative frame** ("Save 30%") | Works better than absolute frame for most price communications |
| **Baseline frame** ("1 in 3 doctors recommend") | Social proof — frames choice as normal, not exceptional |

**In comms:** Always ask: are we framing this as a gain or a loss? Is that the right frame for this audience and this decision?

---

### 4. Loss Aversion

**Mechanism:** Losses are psychologically weighted approximately *twice as heavily* as equivalent gains. Losing €100 hurts more than gaining €100 feels good.

**In comms:**
- Fear of missing out (FOMO) is loss aversion, not aspiration. It's powerful *because* it activates avoidance, not approach motivation.
- Insurance, security, health, and financial products should emphasise what is protected, not what is gained.
- Limited-time offers exploit loss aversion — the ending of the opportunity creates more urgency than the opportunity itself.

**Warning:** Loss framing creates anxiety. In category contexts where anxiety is already high, adding loss framing can cause paralysis rather than action.

---

### 5. Availability Heuristic

**Mechanism:** People estimate the frequency or probability of something by how *easily examples come to mind*. Recent, vivid, emotionally charged, or frequently encountered examples are recalled more easily — and therefore feel more common.

**In comms:**
- Frequent exposure makes a brand feel familiar, trustworthy, and widespread — regardless of actual market share.
- Share dramatic, specific, emotionally engaging proof points rather than statistical averages.
- The most recently seen competitor ad shapes how your ad will be compared.

**In media planning:** Being consistently present in the media environment (even at low weight) beats infrequent heavy bursts for building availability. Byron Sharp's principle of reach aligns with this.

---

### 6. Halo Effect

**Mechanism:** One strong positive attribute biases the assessment of all other attributes. We generalise from single data points to form global judgments.

**In comms:**
- Lead with the single most credible, differentiating proof point. Everything else will benefit from the halo it creates.
- A design that looks premium makes the product feel premium.
- A celebrity spokesperson transfers their halo to the brand — whether or not there is any logical connection.

**Warning:** The halo effect operates in reverse too. A single credibility failure contaminates everything. Protect the most important signal.

---

### 7. WYSIATI — What You See Is All There Is

**Mechanism:** System 1 builds a *coherent story* from the information it has available — without noticing what is absent. People don't naturally ask "what am I not being told?" They accept the story the available information permits.

**In comms:**
- Never assume the audience has context you haven't given them. They build their story from what's in front of them.
- A brief that omits the competitive context will produce strategy that ignores it.
- An ad that doesn't state the obvious (category, use occasion, what the product does) risks being interpreted through whatever story System 1 constructs from the available signals.

---

### 8. Priming

**Mechanism:** Exposure to one stimulus influences the response to subsequent stimuli — even when the connection is unconscious. Primes can be images, words, sounds, or environmental cues.

**In comms:**
- The editorial context in which an ad appears primes its reception. A health story primes health-related brand associations.
- Campaign sequencing matters: what the audience has seen before shapes how they receive what comes next.
- Brand environments (the store, the website, the event space) prime purchase decisions before any message is communicated.

---

### 9. Peak-End Rule

**Mechanism:** People's memory of an experience is dominated by two moments: the *peak* (the most intense positive or negative moment) and the *end*. Duration and average quality have almost no effect on remembered satisfaction.

**In comms and experience design:**
- Design the campaign's most emotionally powerful moment deliberately — don't let it be accidental.
- The end of a campaign, an event, a customer service call, or a piece of content is disproportionately influential on what people remember and tell others.
- In content marketing: nail the final 10 seconds of a video, not just the opening.

---

### 10. Narrative Fallacy

**Mechanism:** Humans compulsively construct *stories* to explain events. We attribute causality to sequences, invent intentions for patterns, and prefer a coherent narrative over a probabilistically accurate account — even when the narrative is fabricated.

**In comms:**
- Stories beat statistics — not because audiences are irrational, but because stories are how System 1 stores and retrieves information.
- Brand heritage narratives are powerful because they give System 1 a *story about why the brand is good* — which is more durable than a list of features.
- Origin stories, founder stories, customer stories activate the narrative network. Use them.

**Warning:** The same mechanism that makes stories persuasive also makes brands vulnerable to counter-narratives. A well-told competitor story can displace your brand story from memory.

## Quick Application Matrix

| Brief type | Primary bias to leverage | Primary bias to avoid triggering |
|-----------|------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Luxury / premium launch | Anchoring + Halo effect | Availability (don't over-expose early) |
| Low-cost / value | Framing (gain) + Cognitive ease | Loss aversion (avoid anxiety) |
| Health / safety | Loss aversion + Availability | Narrative fallacy (don't over-claim) |
| B2B enterprise | Halo + WYSIATI | Framing (avoid loss frame — creates risk aversion at purchase) |
| Brand relaunch | Cognitive ease + Priming | WYSIATI (assume nothing carries over) |
| New category / behaviour change | Availability + Priming | Anchoring against wrong reference |
